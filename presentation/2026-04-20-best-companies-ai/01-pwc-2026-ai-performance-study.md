# PwC 2026 AI Performance Study

**Publisher:** PwC (global press release)
**Date:** 13 April 2026
**Canonical URL:** <https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/news-room/press-releases/2026/pwc-2026-ai-performance-study.html>
**Related landing page:** <https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/services/ai/ai-jobs-barometer.html>

> Headline: **Three-quarters of AI's economic gains are being captured by just 20% of companies — with the leading companies focused on growth, not just productivity.**

## Methodology

- **1,217 senior executives** (director-level and above) surveyed, primarily at large, publicly listed companies.
- **25 sectors**, multiple global regions.
- AI-driven performance measured as **revenue and efficiency gains attributable to AI, adjusted against industry medians**.
- The study analysed **60 management and investment practices**, grouped into two macro-areas — **"AI use"** (how AI is applied to the business) and **"AI foundations"** (infrastructure, governance, data, talent). These form PwC's **AI Fitness Index**, comprising **nine categories** across strategy, governance, data, talent, technology, and innovation capabilities.
- **Companion report:** *Want AI ROI? Go for growth.* — <https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/technology/ai-performance/want-ai-roi-go-for-growth.html>
- **PDF:** <https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/so-you-can/2026/content/roi-from-ai.pdf>

## Headline statistics

| Metric | Finding |
|---|---|
| Share of AI economic value captured by top 20% of firms | **~74%** (80% of companies share the remaining 26%) |
| AI-driven financial performance of most AI-fit firms vs peers | **7.2×** higher |
| Profit-margin gap between leaders and competitors | **+4 percentage points** |
| Share of revenue leaders allocate to AI vs peers | **2.5×** more |
| Leaders with formal processes to scale AI innovations | **3×** more likely |
| Leaders using AI for business-model reinvention | **2.6×** more likely |
| Leaders competing in entirely new markets | **3×** as likely |
| Leaders redesigning workflows around AI (vs just adding AI tools) | **2×** as likely |
| Leaders using AI to identify/pursue growth opportunities | **2–3×** more likely |
| Leaders increasing decisions made **without human intervention** | **2.8×** as likely |
| Companies operating in autonomous, self-optimising ways | **1.9×** more likely |
| Leaders executing multiple tasks **within guardrails** | **1.8×** as likely |
| Leaders with a **Responsible AI framework** guiding strategy | **1.6×** as likely |
| Employees at top-tier firms trusting AI outputs | **2.1×** more likely |
| Leaders providing role-specific AI training | **2×** as likely |

## Key findings

1. **Leaders treat AI as a growth engine, not a cost-reduction tool.** They pursue business-model reinvention and industry-convergence opportunities.
2. **Innovation culture matters more than tool count.** AI leaders use AI as a vehicle for constant experimentation and establish centralised innovation hubs to scale breakthroughs across departments.
3. **AI-fit organisations improve intermediate performance outcomes** — decision-making quality, customer-service offerings, and business-model clarity — and those improvements then translate into financial results.
4. **Trust and governance are enablers, not blockers.** Strong ethical guidelines and governance frameworks raise employee confidence, which enables the radical organisational change that delivers AI value.
5. **Leaders invest in workforce capability** so that humans can innovate alongside the technology, not just consume it.

## Key quote

> "Many companies are busy rolling out AI pilots, but only a minority are converting that activity into measurable financial returns. The leaders stand out because they point AI at growth, not just cost reduction, and back that ambition with the foundations that make AI scalable and reliable."
> — Joe Atkinson, global chief AI officer, PwC
