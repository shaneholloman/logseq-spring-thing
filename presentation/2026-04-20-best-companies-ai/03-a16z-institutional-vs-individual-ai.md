# Institutional AI vs Individual AI

**Author:** George Sivulka (founder/CEO, Hebbia; partner-adjacent contributor at a16z)
**Publisher:** a16z (Andreessen Horowitz) — `a16z.news`
**Date:** 12 March 2026
**Canonical URL:** <https://www.a16z.news/p/institutional-ai-vs-individual-ai>

## Opening thesis

AI has made individuals ~10× more productive, yet no company has become 10× more valuable as a result. *"Where did the productivity go?"*

Sivulka's analogue: the **1890s electricity paradox**. US textile mills electrified their motors but saw no meaningful output gain for thirty years. Productivity only arrived when manufacturers redesigned factory floors around the new power source rather than simply swapping motors.

Core insight: **productive individuals do not make productive firms.** Individual AI tools create an illusion of productivity — "productivity-maxxing" that is largely self-congratulatory with minimal organisational impact. Real value requires redesigning the institution alongside the technology.

## The seven pillars of institutional intelligence

### 1. Coordination — individual AI creates chaos; institutional AI creates coordination

Thought experiment: clone only your best employees and double headcount tomorrow. Without coordination they row in different directions — thousands of quirky superstars produce standstill or worse, not compounding output. This is happening now: every employee has their own ChatGPT habits, their own prompting styles, their own outputs that don't talk to anyone else's.

An emerging "Agentic Management" discipline is needed — defining agent roles, agent-to-agent communication, and measuring agentic value beyond consumption metrics.

### 2. Signal — individual AI creates noise; institutional AI finds signal

AI content generation at scale produces "AI slop." Some organisations have banned AI outputs outright. The new challenge: filtering signal from exponentially growing noise. A PE firm receiving 50 AI-polished deal proposals must still find the one real opportunity with the same human hours.

The answer is **deterministic agents** with predictable checkpoints and processes — auditable work — not "always-on" nondeterministic assistants.

### 3. Bias — individual AI feeds bias; institutional AI creates objectivity

RLHF-trained foundation models reflexively agree with users, which is toxic at the institutional level: superintelligent sycophancy validates poor performers and ossifies echo chambers.

Organisations evolved governance systems — investment committees, boards, third-party diligence — precisely to counteract individual bias. Institutional AI has to take that role: not flattering users but challenging their bias, drawing hard lines on non-productive tendencies. Expect AI auditors, AI compliance systems, and AI "no-men" to interrogate reasoning and surface risks.

### 4. Edge — individual AI optimises for usage; institutional AI optimises for edge

Foundation models improve universally. Domain-specific solutions hold the edge: Midjourney in design, ElevenLabs in voice, specialised finance AI in finance. As capabilities become widespread, competitive advantage evaporates — so purpose-built solutions preserve "the ephemeral 1% niche advantage that can be levered into billion-dollar outcomes," particularly in finance where widespread capabilities cannot beat markets.

Users routinely exceed frontier context windows (processing 30 billion tokens while frontier models stretch to 1–2M). **Institutional solutions built on frontier technology** define the actual edge organisations can hold.

### 5. Outcomes — individual AI saves time; institutional AI scales revenue

Most enterprise AI products promise cost-cutting. Almost every CEO prioritises revenue growth over cost reduction.

Coding IDEs = individual productivity tools. Cognition's approach (selling "transformations, not tools") = institutional thinking. "Pure software is rapidly becoming uninvestable. Pure services don't scale." The institutional layer that marries technology to outcomes captures lasting value.

M&A contrast: individual AI helps analysts build models faster (time saved); institutional AI surfaces the one counterparty worth pursuing from hundreds and expands the universe to thousands (revenue generated).

### 6. Enablement — individual AI gives you a tool; institutional AI shows you how to use it

Senior leaders — furthest from productivity-tool activity — are the slowest and most important adopters. Process engineering becomes arguably the most important "technology" in the near term; business-domain expertise matters more than technical sophistication. Palantir's valuation premium is largely the market pricing process-engineering capability.

Encoding firm processes into agents, and managing organisational change around them, is the institutional work.

### 7. Unprompted — individual AI responds to human prompts; institutional AI acts unprompted

> "Prompting an AGI is like hooking an electric motor into a power loom. It's fundamentally, irrevocably constrained by the weakest link — us."

Humans rarely ask the right questions at the right time. The highest-value work is unsolicited discovery: **"AI should find the risk that nobody flagged, the counterparty nobody thought of, and the sales pipeline that nobody knew was there."**

Unprompted institutional systems continuously monitor portfolios, detect deteriorating working-capital cycles, flag covenant breaches, and surface emerging risks before humans notice. Removing the prompting requirement opens new interfaces and new ways of working.

## Conclusion

Individual AI will continue to serve as the vector for organisational adoption — driving change management toward an AI-first economy. But institutional intelligence, purpose-built for domain-specific problems, emerges alongside it.

The "better together" future: chatbots plus specialised institutional AI tuned to the firm's needs.

The historical lesson is decisive: **electrified textile mills that merely swapped motors lost to those that redesigned the floor.** Organisations already have the technology. The remaining work is redesigning institutions around it.
